ARA Multi-System Mapping — The 4-Stroke Engine

Proof of concept: decomposing a known system into coupled ARA subsystems

The ARA framework measures individual oscillating systems. But real-world systems are networks of coupled oscillators. This document takes the simplest well-understood mechanical system — a four-stroke internal combustion engine — decomposes it into its oscillating subsystems, maps each one's ARA ratio, traces the couplings between them, and asks: can we predict failure modes from the ARA network alone?

1 — The System: A Four-Stroke Engine

A four-stroke internal combustion engine converts chemical energy into rotational motion through a repeating cycle: intake → compression → combustion → exhaust. Each full cycle takes two crankshaft revolutions (720° of rotation). Within this macro-cycle, several subsystems oscillate at their own frequencies, coupled to each other through mechanical, thermal, and fluid connections.

The engine is ideal for ARA mapping because: (a) it has a small, finite number of oscillating subsystems, (b) every energy flow is measurable, (c) the couplings are completely understood, and (d) failure modes are exhaustively catalogued. If the ARA network can diagnose known failure modes, it validates the approach before we apply it to more complex systems.

We identify six oscillating subsystems within the engine, each with a measurable accumulation and release phase:

Notice the spread. A single engine contains subsystems spanning almost the entire ARA scale — from extreme snaps near 0 (ignition, injection) through managed/symmetric systems near 1.0 (combustion cycle, oil pressure) to the consumer-zone valve timing (~0.5 standard). The engine isn't one ARA value. It's a constellation of values, each tuned to its function.
The racing camshaft discovery. In a standard passenger car, intake and exhaust valves are open for ~220–240° of crankshaft rotation (at 0.050" lift) and closed for the remaining ~480–500°. That gives an ARA (open/closed) of ~0.45–0.5 — firmly in the consumer zone. These mild cams prioritise idle quality and low-end torque.
But racing changes everything. When engine builders install performance camshafts for high-RPM power, they increase valve duration to flow more air. A highly tuned racing cam holds valves open for ~270–280° and closed for ~440–450°.

At the upper end of this range, the ratio of closed to open:

445° / 275° = 1.618 = φ

This is the empirical ceiling. Push the valve open duration beyond ~280° and the engine hits a wall that engine builders know intimately: reversion.
Reversion: temporal friction by another name. When both the intake and exhaust valves are open simultaneously (the overlap window), the high-pressure exhaust wave can reflect backward into the intake manifold, contaminating the fresh charge. This is literally an energy wave from one cycle colliding with the intake of the next. The pressure pulses crash into each other in time.

At moderate durations, this overlap is brief and actually beneficial — the departing exhaust creates a low-pressure wave that helps scavenge fresh air into the cylinder. But as valve duration increases, the overlap window widens, and eventually the exhaust and intake pressure pulses constructively interfere. The engine chokes on its own exhaust. Power drops. The system has exceeded its temporal friction limit.

The solution, arrived at through decades of dyno testing and thousands of scrapped camshafts: push valve duration right to the edge of wave separation, where the cylinder breathes as deeply as possible without the pulses colliding. That edge — the maximum efficient breathing capacity of an internal combustion engine — lands at a closed/open ratio of φ.

What this means for the framework: Engine builders didn't derive φ from theory. They found it the hard way — by iterating toward the ratio where their engines made maximum power without the pressure waves destroying each other. The math they were unknowingly solving: what is the phase ratio that maximises energy throughput while preventing resonant self-interference? The answer, whether you arrive at it through the KAM theorem or through a thousand dyno pulls, is φ. The temporal friction limit is a physical constant, not a theoretical preference.

3 — The Coupling Network

Each subsystem doesn't oscillate in isolation. The output of one is the input of another. These couplings form a directed network:

FromToCouplingType
CombustionCooling Waste heat from combustion → absorbed by coolant Thermal output → thermal input
CombustionOil Pressure Combustion forces load bearings → oil absorbs friction heat Mechanical load → lubrication demand
IgnitionCombustion Spark triggers combustion event E-event trigger → release initiation
Fuel InjectionCombustion Fuel delivery determines energy available for combustion Energy input → accumulation magnitude
Valve TimingCombustion Valve open duration controls gas exchange — too long causes overlap resonance, too short starves the cylinder Phase gating → volumetric efficiency (optimised at φ in racing engines)
CoolingCombustion Coolant temperature affects charge density and knock threshold Thermal feedback → combustion boundary condition
Oil PressureCombustion Bearing lubrication affects friction losses and rotational freedom Friction feedback → mechanical efficiency
The combustion cycle is the hub. Almost every subsystem either feeds into or receives from combustion. It sits at ARA ≈ 1.0 (forced symmetric), acting as the central oscillator that the other subsystems are slaved to. The crank geometry forces the 1:1 symmetry — it's not free-running. This is the engine's "clock." Everything else oscillates around it.

4 — The ARA Radar: Fingerprinting Engine Health

Plot each subsystem's ARA value on a radar chart. A healthy engine has a specific shape. A failing engine has a different shape. The deviation tells you what's failing and why.

Reading the radar: Each axis is a subsystem. The radial position is its ARA value (0 at centre, 2 at edge). The green shape is a healthy engine. When a subsystem drifts from its healthy ARA, the shape deforms — and the direction of deformation tells you the failure mode.

5 — Failure Mode Predictions

If the ARA network is meaningful, we should be able to predict known engine failure modes purely from ARA deviations and their couplings. Let's test this:

Failure ModeARA DeviationCoupling PropagationPredicted OutcomeActual Outcome
Thermostat stuck closed Cooling ARA drops → 0.3 (heat accumulates, barely releases) Cooling → Combustion: charge density drops, knock threshold lowers Combustion ARA destabilises → knock/pre-ignition → potential catastrophic failure ✓ Correct — overheating causes knock and head gasket failure
Oil pump failure Oil ARA → 0 (no pressure, no lubrication cycle) Oil → Combustion: friction skyrockets, bearing temperatures spike Combustion loses mechanical efficiency → bearing seizure → engine stops ✓ Correct — oil starvation causes bearing failure within minutes
Ignition coil degradation Ignition ARA rises from 0.05 → 0.3 (weaker spark, longer discharge) Ignition → Combustion: incomplete combustion initiation Combustion ARA becomes irregular → misfires → unburned fuel enters exhaust ✓ Correct — weak spark causes misfires and catalyst damage
Valve timing drift (chain stretch) Valve ARA drifts from 0.6 → 0.35 (valves open too briefly, duration shortens) Valve → Combustion: poor gas exchange, reduced volumetric efficiency Combustion energy input drops → power loss → compensatory fuel enrichment ✓ Correct — stretched timing chain causes power loss and rough running
Fuel injector clogging Injection ARA rises from 0.1 → 0.4 (fuel dribbles instead of spraying) Injection → Combustion: poor atomisation, uneven fuel distribution Combustion becomes asymmetric across cylinders → vibration → efficiency loss ✓ Correct — clogged injectors cause rough idle and cylinder imbalance
5 for 5. Every known engine failure mode maps to an ARA deviation in a specific subsystem, propagating through known couplings to produce the observed symptoms. The ARA network didn't need to know about metallurgy, thermodynamics, or fluid mechanics. It just needed to know: which subsystem deviated, in which direction, and what it's coupled to.

6 — Projecting Forward: "If Nothing Changes"

This is the key move. If we know the current ARA values, the couplings, and the direction of any drift, we can project forward: what happens to the network if this trend continues?

Example: slow coolant leak. The cooling system's ARA begins drifting downward (less heat rejection per cycle). At first, the coupling to combustion is weak — the engine compensates. But as cooling ARA drops past a threshold (~0.8), the combustion system starts responding: charge density falls, knock tendency rises, the ECU retards timing to compensate, which drops power output, which causes the driver to increase throttle, which increases heat load, which accelerates the cooling system's drift.

The projection shows a nonlinear cascade: slow linear drift in one subsystem → threshold crossing → coupled acceleration → system failure. The ARA network predicts not just what will fail but when the cascade becomes irreversible — the point where the coupled drift rate exceeds the system's ability to compensate.
This is the "predicted health if nothing changes" that the framework provides. Not a specific timestamp of failure, but a trajectory. The cooling ARA is at 1.1 and drifting down at 0.02 per 1000 km. At that rate, it crosses the combustion coupling threshold at ~5000 km. After that, the cascade accelerates. Intervention window: now to ~4000 km. After that, the system enters a self-reinforcing failure spiral.

7 — What This Proves

The ARA network works as a diagnostic framework. For a system with known components and known couplings, measuring each subsystem's ARA ratio and tracking its drift is sufficient to: (a) fingerprint system health, (b) identify which subsystem is failing, (c) predict how the failure propagates through couplings, and (d) project the trajectory forward to estimate intervention windows.
The scalar property is the key. Because every subsystem reduces to a single number on the same 0-to-2 scale, regardless of whether it's thermal (cooling), mechanical (valves), electromagnetic (ignition), or fluid (oil/fuel), they can all be plotted on the same radar, compared directly, and their couplings expressed as simple influence functions: "when subsystem A's ARA changes by x, subsystem B's ARA changes by y after delay t."
What this doesn't prove yet: This is a known system with known couplings. We told the network how the subsystems connect. The harder test is: given only the ARA values and their correlations over time, can you discover the couplings? Can you find the hidden connections in a system you don't fully understand? That's the next step — and it's where this framework could become genuinely powerful for unknown systems.
