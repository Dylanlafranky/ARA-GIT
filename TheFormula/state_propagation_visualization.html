State propagation vs averaged amplitude — ECG comparison

Same data, same anchor (14 h into nsr001), same rungs. The two methods differ only in how they read the rung's current amplitude and project forward.

The two methods, side by side

OLD (averaged-amplitude + cosine): amp = std of last 50 bandpass samples × √2. ARA = mean over all peak-pairs in training. Cosine forward.

NEW (state propagation): amp = peak in the most recent cycle only. ARA = measured on the most recent 1-2 cycles only. ARA-shaped piecewise wave forward (asymmetric build vs release).

2 · Per-horizon metrics across 16 anchors (12 h → 20 h every 30 min)

The state-propagation approach flips the sign of correlation at 30 min (−0.136 → +0.049) and gives small MAE improvements at most horizons. But R²(pers) is strongly negative across the board for both methods — persistence wins everywhere here, which means the predictions are flatter than the actual data.

3 · What this is telling us

Your intuition was right: averaging amplitude over the whole training window is too sluggish. State-propagation does pick up some lift, especially at mid-horizon. But the deeper issue isn't fixed — both methods are too damped against an ECG that's actually quite spiky in places.

The likely deeper fix isn't single-step vs averaging — it's not summing across rungs at long lead. When we add up bandpass components from 12 different rungs each with small amplitude, the sum lives near the mean. What we probably want at any moment is only the dominant rung's contribution plus the last observed value as the anchor.

A clean version of your idea would: pick the rung that's currently carrying the most energy, use its state (v_now, delta, amp_local, ARA_local), step forward with an ARA-shaped wave starting from v_now itself, ignore the smaller rungs unless you specifically want their decoration.
