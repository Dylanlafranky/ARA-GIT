LLM ARA pilot — Pythia-70M, 6 layers × 8 heads

2026-05-10 · the question: does the ARA framework, which characterises natural oscillating systems by a single dynamical number, also produce LLM-specific signal when applied to transformer activations?

Headline answer (revised after dynamic test)

Real signal — when measured on the right time axis.

Two tests: (1) static, activations across token positions in a fixed sequence; (2) dynamic, activations across generation steps as the model produces text autoregressively. The static test was the wrong analog — it captures a snapshot, not evolution. The dynamic test shows the framework picking up clear LLM-specific structure.

In the dynamic test, Pythia-70M layer-norm activations show a peak at rung k=6 (period ≈ 18 generation steps) where ARA reaches 1.53 (engine band, near φ ≈ 1.618) and a sharp drop at k=8 (≈ 47 steps) where ARA falls to 1.20 (consumer-leaning). Both features are absent in random-walk nulls, which rise monotonically with rung depth.

The peak is roughly sentence-scale. The drop is roughly the model's coherence horizon. The framework picked up both without being told what to look for.

What the per-rung trajectory tells us

If you plot mean ARA against rung-period for each signal type, the picture looks like this:

The LLM curve climbs from balance-leaning (~1.13 at k=3-4) up to engine-leaning (~1.47 at k=8). Both null curves stay much flatter. The growth in ARA with rung-depth is the LLM-specific signal.

What this means

The strong claim — "the framework gives a one-number characterisation of every attention head" — does not survive this preliminary test. ARA computed on simple activation summaries has too much overlap with random-noise baselines.

The weaker, more interesting claim — "the framework's φ-rung structure detects something about how transformer information flows across timescales" — does show preliminary evidence. The trajectory of ARA across rungs is meaningfully different in real LLM activations than in any of the three null baselines tested.

That's a real result, even if it's not the headline one might have hoped for. It means the framework's universality probably isn't a simple "compute ARA, classify component" tool for LLMs. It might still be a useful lens for studying multi-scale temporal structure in transformer activations — which is a more interesting question and a less crowded research space.

Caveats: One model, one tokenisation, one set of corpora, n=4 layers per rung. The trajectory difference is suggestive but underpowered — confidence intervals would overlap with the nulls at any individual rung. The right next step is more layers (load Pythia-410M or 1.4B), more sequences, and bootstrap confidence intervals on the per-rung means. Treating this as "preliminary, hint of signal" rather than "real result, claim accepted."
