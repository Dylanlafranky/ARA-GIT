Pythia size-series coupling-graph analysis

2026-05-10 · same prompt, same seed, four model sizes. Framework's coupling-graph approach applied at each. The question: does coupling structure phase-transition somewhere in the size series?

It does — but the transition tracks architectural depth, not parameter count. Pythia-410M (24 layers) has the strongest hierarchical coupling structure: within-layer correlation 1.51× across-layer (vs 1.03–1.07 in the other three sizes), spectral decay 0.42 (vs 0.15–0.22), and 3520 anti-phase pairs (vs 48–1408). Pythia-1B has more parameters but only 16 layers — and its coupling structure drops back to the 70M/160M baseline.

What changes between 160M and 410M (one φ²-rung step)

Two of the three metrics (within/across ratio, spectral decay) peak at 410M and drop back at 1B. The third (alive fraction) keeps rising with size. Suggests "richness" scales monotonically but "hierarchical organisation" depends on layer depth specifically.

Pythia size spacing in φ-rungs

Pythia model sizes are spaced almost exactly at φ² = 2.618 (the matched-rung pair distance):

stepsize ratioφ-power
70M → 160M2.29φ^1.72
160M → 410M2.56φ^1.96
410M → 1B2.44φ^1.85

The transition we measured occurs between 160M and 410M — a single φ²-rung step. But the 70M→160M step is also one φ²-rung and shows no transition. So some rung crossings produce phase changes and others don't. The architectural shape (24 layers at 410M, max in this series) appears to be what enables the matched-rung hierarchy to form.

What this could mean

  • Coupling structure isn't a simple function of parameter count. 1B has 2.4× the parameters of 410M and shows weaker hierarchical structure. The standard "more params = more emergent capability" picture has a wrinkle.
  • Layer depth gives the network its φ-rungs. The framework's prediction is that hierarchy needs at least one matched-rung pair distance (φ² ≈ 2.6 rungs minimum) to form. With 6 layers (70M) you barely have room. With 12 (160M) you have a tight fit. With 24 (410M) you have ~9 φ²-rungs of room, enough for rich matched-rung structure. With 16 (1B) you regress.
  • This is a testable hypothesis with a concrete prediction. A 24-layer model at any width should show this signature. Pythia-1.4B (24 layers, 2048 hidden) would be the natural confirmation: same depth as 410M, larger width, should show the same hierarchical coupling.
  • For interpretability research: deep-but-narrow models might be more interpretable than wide-but-shallow ones, because their coupling structure is more hierarchically organised.

The honest framing

This is one experiment, one prompt, one seed. The headline finding (depth-not-width drives coupling structure) needs:

  • Pythia-1.4B test (24 layers, larger width) — predicts strong framework signature
  • Pythia-2.8B test (32 layers, even deeper) — predicts even stronger framework signature
  • Multiple seeds and prompts to confirm robustness
  • Comparison against other model families with different aspect ratios
Caveats: n=4 model sizes from one family is far too small to claim a general law. The within/across ratio difference between 410M and the others is large but the underlying correlations are themselves driven by global activation drift; partial correlation analysis would tighten the claim. Also: the alive% metric scales monotonically with size, suggesting "more components active during generation" is real but doesn't itself imply hierarchy. The hierarchy claim rests on the within/across ratio and spectral decay specifically, both of which need more replicates.

Significance

If the depth-tracking hypothesis holds up under further testing, it would offer:

  1. A measurement-based mechanistic account for why some architectures generalise better than others at fixed parameter count.
  2. A cheap diagnostic for "is this architecture going to develop hierarchical internal structure?" without having to fully train the model.
  3. A bridge between the framework's matched-rung-pair concept and concrete LLM internals: depth is what gives the network its φ-rungs.
