Drain architecture vs averaged amplitude — ECG nsr001

Anchor at 14 h. Comparison of two forward-projection architectures over 16 rolling anchors and 6 horizons (1 min → 2 h).

Headline at 1 min lead: the drain architecture cuts MAE from 219.2 ms → 149.1 ms (a 32% drop) and lifts R²(pers) from −2.21 to −0.53. At 5 min, correlation rises from +0.284 to +0.359. The lift converges out at longer horizons.

The two architectures, side by side

OLD (averaged amplitude + cosine): Each rung's amplitude = std of last 50 bandpass samples × √2. Forward = ARA-driven cosine decay. Final prediction = mean_train + Σ rung_weight × cosine. Ignores v_now entirely.

DRAIN (Dylan's architecture): Anchor at v_now (the last observed value). Step forward sample by sample, drain rate (1/φ + (π−3)/π) / period_home ≈ 0.0014 per step pulls v gently toward mean_train + log-weighted rung sum. Log-weights by distance from data-home rung k=8. All rungs contribute their full instantaneous amplitude (no kappa decay).

The drain architecture is a clean win at 1 min and 5 min. At 15 min and beyond, the prediction has had time to relax toward the structured target — at that point both methods are doing roughly the same thing.

3 · Why this works at short lead

Heart-rate is sticky in the short term. If the last beat was 750 ms, the next is much more likely to be near 750 ms than to be 660 ms (the mean). The OLD method ignores this and starts every prediction from the mean. The DRAIN architecture starts at v_now, then bleeds toward the mean at a φ + π-leak rate.

For the first ~30 min, that anchoring captures real autoregressive structure. The MAE drops ~30% at 1 min. Past that, the drain has had enough steps to fully relax v to the structured target, and the two methods converge.

To extend the lift to longer horizons we'd need to let more energy through at the rung level — bigger structured-target swings rather than the small bandpass amplitudes we currently get. That's the next step.
