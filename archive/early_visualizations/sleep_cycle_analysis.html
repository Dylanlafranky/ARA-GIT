Accumulation-release dynamics in human sleep architecture

The System: Human Sleep Architecture

Human sleep cycles through NREM (Non-Rapid Eye Movement) and REM (Rapid Eye Movement) stages in ~90-minute ultradian cycles, repeating 4-5 times per night. NREM is the accumulation phase — the brain consolidates memory, clears metabolic waste, and restores energy. REM is the release phase — intense neural activity, dreaming, emotional processing.

The key architectural feature: the NREM/REM ratio shifts across the night. Early cycles are NREM-dominated (deep slow-wave sleep, short REM). Late cycles flip: shallow NREM, long REM. The whole-night average is ~75-80% NREM / 20-25% REM.

φ Test 3: The φ-Crossover — Where Does the Ratio Hit 1.618?

If the NREM/REM ratio declines monotonically from ~7.0 (Cycle 1) to ~0.91 (Cycle 5), it must pass through exactly φ at some point during the night. When?

★ FRAMEWORK PREDICTION: The φ-Crossover

Interpolating between Cycle 3 (ratio 2.33) and Cycle 4 (ratio 1.50), the NREM/REM ratio crosses exactly φ = 1.618 approximately 3.5-4 hours into sleep — right at the transition from the "deep sleep dominated" first half to the "REM dominated" second half.

This is the pivot point of the night. Before this moment, the brain prioritises physical restoration (deep NREM). After it, the brain prioritises cognitive/emotional processing (REM). φ marks the transition between the two fundamental modes of sleep.

At the φ-crossover: NREM ≈ 62% of the cycle, REM ≈ 38%. The same 62/38 split as the healthy heart (diastole/systole), ENSO (La Niña/El Niño), and short-period Cepheids (decline/rise).

e — Process S: The Exponential Envelope

Borbély's Process S is one of the cleanest exponential dynamics in all of biology. Sleep pressure (measured by EEG slow-wave activity) follows:

S(t) = S∞ + (S0 − S∞) · e−t/τ

The time constant τ ≈ 1.7-2.5 hours for the exponential decline during sleep. During waking, adenosine accumulates exponentially. During sleep, it clears exponentially. This is e governing the growth/decay envelope of the accumulation-release cycle.

✓ STRONG HIT — Exponential dynamics

Process S is a textbook example of exponential accumulation-release dynamics relevant to the ARA framework. Adenosine builds exponentially, crosses threshold, triggers sleep onset, then decays exponentially during sleep until it crosses the wake threshold. The exponential envelope governs the timing and intensity of the accumulation-release cycle.

OVERALL VERDICT: NOVEL FINDING: φ as transition marker

Sleep doesn't show φ the same way the heart does (a stable resting ratio). Instead, φ appears as a transition marker — the point where the brain pivots from physical restoration to cognitive/emotional processing. This is actually a new mode of φ-appearance in the framework:

Heart: φ is a stable equilibrium ratio (diastole/systole = 1.618 at rest).
Sleep: φ is a dynamic crossover point (NREM/REM ratio passes through 1.618 mid-night).
Both: φ marks the boundary between accumulation and release dominance.

The Cardiac Parallel: Sleep as a Macro-Heartbeat

The analogy is almost too clean. The heart has diastole (filling/accumulation) and systole (ejection/release) at a ratio of φ. Sleep has NREM (restoration/accumulation) and REM (processing/release) — and when the phases are comparable in duration (~Cycle 4), the ratio approaches φ.

Both systems show the same disease pattern: disruption of the natural ratio predicts pathology. Heart failure distorts diastole/systole away from φ. Sleep disorders distort NREM/REM architecture. In both cases, the system has a "target" ratio it defends homeostatically (REM rebound ≈ the Frank-Starling mechanism).

The difference: the heart maintains φ as a steady state at each beat. Sleep traverses φ once per night as it transitions between modes. One is a standing wave at φ; the other is a wave that passes through φ. Both are the same architecture operating at different timescales.
