The Geometry of Time

A heuristic framework for classifying non-equilibrium thermodynamic systems by their accumulation–release phase ratio

How river basins, human biomechanics, and dying stars reveal a universal 0-to-2 phase-ratio scale for complex systems

Dylan La Franchi
Heuristic Framework — Open for Falsification
Abstract

Cyclical systems in nature — from river basins to human lungs to stellar dynamos — operate as relaxation oscillators, spending measurable time accumulating energy and measurable time releasing it. By computing a single dimensionless metric, the Accumulation–Release Asymmetry (ARA) ratio, across publicly available datasets in biology, earth science, and astrophysics, a consistent taxonomy emerges. Systems cluster into discrete bands on a 0-to-2 scale, with the golden ratio (φ ≈ 1.618) marking the attractor for unforced, self-organising engines. Deviation from this ratio correlates with external forcing, managed intervention, or pathological degradation. This paper presents the heuristic framework, the supporting data, an honest account of its limitations, and an invitation to falsification.

1. Introduction: The Problem with Symmetry

In idealised physics, we often look for perfect symmetry. But in the physical universe — driven by the tension between gravitational accumulation and entropic dissipation — natural systems almost never operate at a 1:1 ratio. The heart spends longer in diastole than systole. Rivers rise faster than they fall. Stars brighten more sharply than they dim.

Whether examining a cardiac cycle, a storm hydrograph, or a solar dynamo, cyclical systems behave as relaxation oscillators: they accumulate energy (tension, pressure, charge) over some duration Taccumulation, then release it over a distinct duration Trelease. These two durations are almost never equal.

The core observation of this framework is that the ratio between these durations is not arbitrary. Across multiple independent domains, a single dimensionless number appears to govern where self-organising systems settle:

ARA = Trelease / Taccumulation
The Accumulation–Release Asymmetry ratio

What emerges is not a mystical invocation of the golden ratio, but a falsifiable observation: free-running thermodynamic systems converge on φ ≈ 1.618, and measurable deviation from this value diagnoses the degree of external forcing, constraint, or pathology acting on the system.

2. Why φ? A Mathematical, Not Mystical, Argument

The golden ratio carries significant cultural baggage. It has been over-claimed in art, architecture, and popular science to the point where invoking it in a serious framework risks immediate dismissal. This concern is legitimate, and it is one of the primary reasons this work is being released for open review rather than presented as settled science.

However, φ has a specific, well-established mathematical property that makes it uniquely relevant to oscillating systems: it is the most irrational number. Its continued fraction expansion is [1; 1, 1, 1, ...] — the slowest-converging of all irrational numbers. This means it is the number least expressible as a ratio of integers.

In physical systems, this property has a direct consequence: φ-spacing prevents destructive resonance. When recursive cycles pack at a ratio of φ, no cycle ever perfectly overlaps a previous one. Energy cannot accumulate at a single harmonic. This is not speculation — it is the same principle behind φ-phyllotaxis in botany, where sunflower seeds and leaf arrangements use φ-packing to maximise spatial distribution with minimum overlap.

The proposal here is that what biology does with φ in space — optimal packing — free-running thermodynamic engines do with φ in time: optimal distribution of energy between accumulation and release phases, preventing bottlenecks and destructive interference between successive cycles.

Honest Limitation

The human brain is a pattern-matching engine. Given enough systems and enough ratios, it is statistically possible to find φ by coincidence. The critical question is whether φ appears preferentially in free-running self-organising systems and is absent in forced or managed systems — and whether that distinction is predictable in advance. The data presented below attempts to address this by including both positive results and deliberate negative controls.

4.2 Resting Human Breath (Exhalation / Inhalation)

Using the NeuroKit2 resting physiological dataset (5 minutes at 100 Hz), respiratory phase durations were extracted after standard bandpass cleaning (0.05–0.35 Hz). The exhalation/inhalation duration ratio across 93 complete breath cycles yielded a median of 1.61 — within 0.5% of φ.

Resting breath is governed by chemoreceptor thresholds, not a central clock. The diaphragm fires when CO2 partial pressure exceeds a chemical tipping point, making it a genuinely free-running thermodynamic oscillator. The proximity to φ is consistent with a system that has found its own optimal accumulation–release balance.

4.3 Natural Watershed Hydrographs

The USDA Soil Conservation Service Dimensionless Unit Hydrograph — a standard derived from aggregating thousands of natural US watershed responses — has a recession-to-peak ratio of 1.67, within 3.2% of φ, and a volume split of 62.5% / 37.5%.

Direct analysis of USGS 15-minute streamflow data using Lyne-Hollick baseflow separation confirmed this pattern. An important methodological note: initial analysis using daily data without baseflow separation produced a median of 3.0 — appearing to falsify the prediction. This was resolved by isolating the direct storm runoff signal from the slow groundwater drainage tail, yielding a mean of 1.554 (3.9% from φ). The lesson: measurement boundaries matter. The system boundary must match the physical process being measured.

4.4 Human Gait: The Full Locomotion Arc

Analysis of the PhysioNet gaitndd database (300 Hz foot-force sensors, 16 healthy controls, 8,591 strides) yielded a walking stance/swing median of 1.355. This initially appeared to miss φ by 16.3%.

However, walking gait is not a free-running system — it is constrained by spinal central pattern generators (biological pacemakers) and gravity. The framework predicts that constrained systems sit below φ on the ARA scale, proportional to their degree of constraint. A median of 1.355 is consistent with this prediction.

The critical insight emerged from mapping stance/swing ratios across the full range of human locomotion speeds, using peer-reviewed biomechanics data from eight independent sources. The resulting curve reveals three structurally significant thresholds:

Key Finding — The Locomotion Arc

Ratio = φ within the preferred walking speed range. Interpolating from the literature data, the stance/swing ratio crosses through φ ≈ 1.618 at approximately 1.25–1.30 m/s. This falls within the published range for self-selected preferred walking speed (1.2–1.4 m/s; Bohannon 1997). At 1.25 m/s the ratio is 1.621 (0.2% from φ); at 1.30 m/s it is 1.597 (1.3% from φ). The exact crossing depends on interpolation between literature data points and should be treated as approximate, but it consistently falls within the preferred speed window across reasonable variations in the source data.

Ratio = 1.0 at 2.20 m/s — this directly matches the published walk-run transition speed (no interpolation required: the literature reports 50%/50% stance/swing at this speed). The point where stance and swing become equal is where humans instinctively switch gait modes. On the ARA scale, this is the sustainability threshold: above this speed, the system becomes net-consumptive.

Ratio = 1/φ at ~3.8 m/s — this approximates the anaerobic threshold. The inverse of φ may mark the boundary of sustainable aerobic locomotion.

A note on the measured baseline: The PhysioNet gaitndd controls (median ratio 1.355) were recorded during overground hallway walking at self-selected speed using force-sensitive insoles (Hausdorff et al., 1998). The dataset records stride timing but not speed directly, so the walking speed of ~1.1 m/s is estimated. The literature curve predicts a ratio of 1.355 at approximately 1.7 m/s (a brisk walk), suggesting the gaitndd controls may have been walking faster than initially estimated — plausible for healthy adults covering a 77-metre hallway. The literature speed-ratio curve and the measured gaitndd baseline are consistent if the actual walking speed was in the 1.5–1.7 m/s range.

4.5 Negative Controls: Where φ Correctly Does Not Appear

A framework that finds φ everywhere is unfalsifiable and therefore unscientific. The following systems were tested specifically because they are not free-running self-organising oscillators. The prediction was that they would not converge on φ.

Arctic sea ice (median 0.91, n=41 annual cycles): The freeze/melt cycle is externally forced by Earth's axial tilt and orbital mechanics. The system has no freedom to find its own balance point. Result: near-symmetric, as predicted.

EEG delta waves (median 1.05, n=150) and beta waves (median 1.00, n=1,335): Brain oscillations in these bands are driven by thalamic pacemaker neurons — biological clocks that impose symmetric timing. Result: ratio ≈ 1.0, as predicted.

Cryptocurrency markets (BTC-USD, median 1.19, n=71): Among the most heavily manipulated, bot-driven, and sentiment-whiplashed systems measurable. No stable self-organisation. Result: noisy, structureless distribution.

Blood glucose clearance (~1.2): A chemically forced homeostatic response, not a self-sustaining cycle. The insulin response is a managed clearing mechanism. Result: overdamped, as predicted.

4.6 Solar Dynamo and Stellar Pulsation

Solar sunspot cycles (SILSO data, 1749–present): The well-known Waldmeier effect — solar cycles rise faster than they decline — produces a Tfall/Trise median of 1.73 across 24 complete cycles.

An important caveat: this ratio assumes sunspot activity represents the Sun's generative output — the productive phase of the solar dynamo. But there is a plausible alternative: sunspots may be better understood as a consumer process — the clearing of accumulated magnetic tension at the solar surface. Under this interpretation, what we observe in the 11-year sunspot cycle is not the engine itself but the exhaust: magnetic flux rises slowly (accumulation of subsurface tension), then erupts rapidly as sunspots, flares, and coronal mass ejections clear it (release as consumption/destruction).

If sunspots are consumers, the phase labels should reverse: the "rise" phase becomes the release (magnetic tension discharging through surface activity), and the "fall" phase becomes accumulation (the quiet Sun rebuilding magnetic structure). This would invert the ratio to Trise/Tfall ≈ 0.58 — placing it in the Consumer zone of the ARA scale, alongside fire and avalanches. The sunspot cycle would then be the Sun's way of periodically clearing magnetic debris, not its productive output.

To measure the Sun as a generator, we would likely need to map the full magnetic dynamo — the 22-year Hale cycle that includes polarity reversal — or the deeper convective processes that drive it. The 11-year sunspot cycle may be only the surface symptom. This remains an open question, and the solar ARA placement should be treated as interpretation-dependent (see Section 6.4).

Cepheid variable star V1154 Cyg (Kepler mission, 432 pulsation cycles): The decline/rise ratio is locked at 2.00 with remarkably low variance. Cepheid pulsation occurs in a deep gravitational potential well — the oscillation is a standing wave with rigid boundary conditions. Under such extreme constraint, the system snaps to the nearest integer harmonic rather than the irrational φ. This is consistent with known wave physics: resonant cavities with fixed boundaries select integer modes.

5. Interpretation: What the Scale Measures

The ARA scale does not measure "health" or "quality" on a simple gradient. It measures the degree of freedom a system has to self-organise its own temporal rhythm.

Systems with maximum freedom (no external clock, no chemical forcing, no rigid boundary conditions) converge on φ. Systems with increasing constraint are displaced from φ — either downward toward 1.0 (pacemaker forcing, symmetric oscillation) or upward toward 2.0 (gravitational/resonant constraint, integer locking).

This explains the paradoxical gait result, where Parkinson's and ALS patients show ratios closer to φ than healthy controls. Healthy walking gait is constrained by spinal central pattern generators — a biological metronome that enables efficient, repetitive locomotion at a ratio of ~1.35. When neurodegenerative disease degrades the CPG, the body suppresses the failing metronome and defaults to a slower, self-balancing gait — the characteristic Parkinsonian shuffle. This compensatory mode, freed from the broken clock, drifts toward φ because φ is the attractor that sits underneath when the pacemaker stops working.

Disease, in this framework, is not a simple displacement away from φ. It is a displacement away from wherever the system's healthy baseline sits on the ARA scale — sometimes toward φ (when constraint is removed), sometimes away from it (when constraint is added).

5.1 The Scale as a Loop, Not a Line

An important structural observation: the ARA scale is not unbounded. Both extremes — 0 and 2 — represent system failure. At 0, the system accumulates infinitely and releases nothing (gravitational collapse). At 2.0, the system is locked into rigid integer resonance with no thermodynamic flexibility. Push beyond 2.0 and the cycle cannot sustain coherence; the system enters a definitive disruption state (an E event, in the language of this framework) and either resets to a new baseline or dissolves entirely.

This suggests the scale is better understood as a closed loop than a linear axis. Both ends curve toward the same outcome: system death. The singularity at 0 is implosive death (pure gravity). The stall beyond 2.0 is resonant death (pure rigidity). φ sits at the apex of the loop — the point of maximum distance from both failure modes, where the system has the greatest capacity to sustain itself against both gravitational collapse and harmonic lock-in.

If this interpretation is correct, the ARA scale is not merely a diagnostic ruler but a description of the viable operating range for non-equilibrium systems. Systems that drift toward either boundary accumulate displacement from their sustainable baseline, and the magnitude of the eventual corrective disruption scales with that accumulated displacement.

6. Known Weaknesses and Counterarguments

6.1 Pattern Matching and Confirmation Bias

The most serious critique of any φ-based framework is that the golden ratio is being projected onto data rather than discovered within it. This risk is real. The human brain, and large language models alike, are prone to finding patterns in noise. The following mitigations have been applied, but the reader should evaluate their sufficiency independently:

All phase ratios were computed algorithmically using standard signal-processing tools (peak detection, Otsu thresholding, baseflow separation). No manual adjustment was applied to bring results closer to φ. Results that missed φ (sea ice, EEG, cryptocurrency, blood glucose, Cepheids) are reported alongside hits. The framework makes falsifiable predictions about which systems should and should not converge on φ, and the negative controls are consistent with those predictions.

6.2 Small Sample Sizes

The solar cycle result (n=24) is the most vulnerable to small-sample criticism. While the Waldmeier effect itself is well-established, 24 complete cycles provide limited statistical power for pinpointing a specific ratio. The breath result (n=93) is stronger but still modest. The gait results (n=8,591+ strides across 16 subjects) are the most statistically robust.

6.3 The Distribution Question

Is φ a sharp attractor (a peak in the distribution) or merely the centre of a broad 1.4–1.8 range? The breath data shows a reasonably sharp peak near φ, but several other results show broader distributions where the median happens to land near φ without a sharp mode. This distinction matters: a sharp peak suggests a genuine attractor, while a broad distribution centred on φ might be coincidental. More data across more systems is needed to resolve this.

6.4 Ratio Direction and Phase Identification (The "Texas Sharpshooter" Problem)

This is the single most important methodological vulnerability in the framework, and it must be addressed head-on rather than papered over.

The ARA ratio requires identifying which phase is "accumulation" and which is "release." If the analyst is free to choose post-hoc — computing the ratio both ways and selecting whichever lands closer to φ — the entire framework becomes unfalsifiable. Any ratio R can be flipped to 1/R or mapped to 2−R, and with enough flexibility, nearly any measurement can be massaged into the 1.0–2.0 range. This is a textbook Texas Sharpshooter fallacy: drawing the target around the bullet hole.

The defence must therefore be structural, not statistical. Phase assignment must be locked in by physical definition before the ratio is computed, and the definition must be independently justifiable — not chosen to produce a convenient number. The tested systems fall into three categories of phase-assignment rigour:

Unambiguous assignment — systems where the physical phases are defined by the measurement itself, with no analyst discretion:

  • Breath: Inhalation vs exhalation. Airflow direction is binary and measured directly. No alternative interpretation exists.
  • Watershed: Hydrograph rising limb vs falling limb. Water level goes up, then comes down. The measurement defines the phases.
  • Sea ice: Freeze season vs melt season. Ice extent increases, then decreases. Satellite measurement defines the crossover.
  • Gait (speed curve): Stance phase vs swing phase, defined by foot-ground contact. A force sensor is either loaded or unloaded.
  • Blood glucose: Post-meal spike vs clearance. Glucose rises above baseline, then returns. The CGM trace defines the inflection.

Physically motivated but arguable assignment — systems where the phase labels follow a standard convention, but an alternative physical interpretation would change the ratio:

  • Solar (sunspot cycles): Conventionally measured as Tfall/Trise (the Waldmeier effect). But this assumes sunspot activity is the generator — the productive output of the solar dynamo. If sunspots are instead a consumer process (clearing accumulated magnetic tension), the phase labels should be reversed. See Section 4.6 for extended discussion.
  • Cepheid pulsation: Measured as decline/rise. The convention follows the light curve shape, but the underlying energy transfer (compression vs expansion of the stellar envelope) could be framed differently.

The honest position: For systems with unambiguous assignment (breath, watershed, sea ice, gait, glucose), the ARA ratio is fully constrained by the physics and cannot be manipulated. These are the framework's strongest results. For systems with arguable assignment (solar, stellar), the ARA placement depends on which physical interpretation is chosen, and the framework must acknowledge this openly rather than claiming a self-correcting geometric rule. Where phase assignment is debatable, the debate itself is informative — it reveals something about whether the system is functioning as a generator or a consumer, which is itself a prediction the framework makes.

6.5 The Cepheid Population Question

The Cepheid test used a single star (V1154 Cyg). Different Cepheids at different periods have different asymmetries. The proper test would examine the decline/rise ratio across many Cepheids as a function of period, testing whether the population-level mean converges on φ as gravitational constraint decreases. This test has not yet been performed.

6.6 Statistical Significance: Monte Carlo Clustering Test

A natural objection is that finding several ratios near φ could be coincidence — the law of large numbers applied to a convenient target. To test this directly, we ran a Monte Carlo simulation: draw 12 random ratios from a null distribution (no structure, no φ attractor), repeat 10 million times, and count how often the result matches or exceeds what we observed.

What we observed: 4 of 12 tested systems fell within 5% of φ (breath, SCS hydrograph, watershed V2, preferred walking speed). 5 of 12 fell within 7% (adding solar).

Across all five null distributions, the clustering is statistically significant at p < 0.05, with most nulls producing p ≈ 0.01. This holds regardless of whether we assume ratios are uniformly distributed, right-skewed, or bell-curved.

The predictive structure test. Clustering alone could reflect post-hoc target selection. The stronger test is whether the hits fall where the framework predicted they would. Of the 12 tested systems, 5 were pre-classified as "predicted hits" (free-running engines: breath, SCS hydrograph, watershed, walking speed, solar) and 7 as "predicted misses" (forced, managed, or constrained systems). All 4 of the 5%-hits came from the 5-member predicted-hit group. The probability of this occurring by chance is:

P = C(5,4) × C(7,0) / C(12,4) = 5/495 ≈ 0.01
Hypergeometric probability: 1 in 99

Combined significance: The joint probability of observing both the clustering and the predictive structure by chance is the product of these independent probabilities — ranging from approximately 1 in 2,300 (most generous null) to 1 in 10,500 (tightest null). The framework is not just finding φ — it is finding it where it predicted it would be and not finding it where it predicted it wouldn't be.

Honest caveat: The 12 systems were not drawn randomly from nature — they were selected by a researcher with a hypothesis. And 12 is a small number. The "predicted hit" classifications could be accused of being defined after early results were seen. These concerns are legitimate, and the proper remedy is independent replication on systems chosen by other researchers. The Monte Carlo test establishes that the pattern is unlikely to be pure noise; it does not establish that the selection process was unbiased.

8. Conclusion and Invitation

What began as a cross-disciplinary observation has converged on a simple, falsifiable proposition: the temporal asymmetry of a non-equilibrium system is not random, and the ratio Trelease / Taccumulation encodes the system's thermodynamic architecture on a continuous 0-to-2 scale.

The golden ratio appears at the centre of this scale not as a mystical constant, but as a mathematical consequence of optimal packing in recursive systems — the temporal equivalent of φ-phyllotaxis. Systems free to self-organise converge there. Systems under external forcing, chemical management, or gravitational constraint are displaced from it in predictable directions.

I am an independent systems thinker. My background is in complex systems — the invisible mathematics that keeps systems balanced between overdamped stagnation and chaotic instability. Living with ME/CFS, a condition defined by the breakdown of biological energy accumulation and release, has provided a daily, visceral lens through which to observe these mechanics.

I am releasing this heuristic framework publicly because a theory is only as good as its ability to survive falsification. I have open-sourced the methodology and the conceptual framework. If you are a data scientist, a fluid dynamicist, a biomechanics researcher, or a complexity scientist, I invite you to test it. Apply these scripts to new datasets. Test the boundaries. Break it if you can.

The geometry of time appears to be written across substrates. I look forward to seeing where else it can be found — and where it fails.
