Section 2
Why It Misses — The Timescale Condition
The puzzle: Old Faithful meets all four conditions for φ to appear:
1. It iterates (erupts repeatedly, thousands of times per year)
2. It self-determines timing (not externally forced)
3. It is threshold-driven (water reaches boiling point at depth, flash-boils, erupts)
4. It has storage-release dynamics (heat accumulates → explosive release)

Yet φ is absent from every metric tested. Why?
Proposed explanation — the comparable-timescales condition:

In every system where φ appears in the accumulation/release ratio, the two phases are on comparable timescales:
• ENSO: ~1.67 years accumulation, ~1 year release (ratio 1.67:1)
• Solar cycles: ~7 years rise, ~4 years decline (ratio ~1.7:1)

In Old Faithful, the phases are on wildly different timescales:
• ~71 minutes accumulation, ~3.5 minutes release (ratio 20:1)
• The eruption is only 4.7% of the cycle

When the release is two orders of magnitude shorter than the accumulation, the system is essentially a capacitor with instantaneous discharge. There is not enough structure in the release phase for φ to shape it. The ratio converges to something set by plumbing geometry and heat transfer rates, not by the medium's geometry.

Refined condition: φ appears in the accumulation/release ratio when both phases occupy a structurally significant fraction of the cycle (roughly >10% each). Systems with extremely asymmetric phase durations show φ only in their event-position structure (φ¹ subdivisions), not in their timing ratios.
Section 3
The Underground Onset Hypothesis
Dylan's key insight: The surface eruption is the tail end of the release, not the beginning. The original analysis measured the surface eruption (3.5 min) as the entire release phase, producing a 20:1 ratio. But the visible eruption is just the final surface expression of a release process that begins much earlier, underground.

If the true release phase starts when the system first begins its pressure cascade at depth, the "release" is much longer than what we see at the surface. The E event we observe is a delayed onset — the tail end of a process that φ may govern from below.

The φ model independently predicts the underground onset at 24.9 minutes before surface eruption.

The broader tremor signal begins ~45 min before eruption, and the GSA eruption model places bubble nucleation at depth ~40 min before eruption. These bracket the φ-predicted onset, suggesting the φ point may mark the critical transition within a longer preparatory process — the moment the system commits to eruption.
Section 5
Testable Predictions
If the underground onset hypothesis is correct:

1. Proportional scaling: The underground onset should always occur at the 61.8% mark of the full cycle, regardless of cycle length. For long eruptions (~85 min cycle), onset at minute ~52.5. For short eruptions (~56 min cycle), onset at minute ~34.6. The onset time should scale linearly with cycle length, maintaining φ proportionality.

2. Tremor peak as φ marker: The seismic tremor peak should consistently fall ~φ² × cycle length minutes before the surface eruption, regardless of whether the eruption is long or short type.

3. Nested subdivision: The underground release phase itself may subdivide by φ — with a slower buildup phase (convective heating) followed by a faster cascade phase (flash boiling propagation), transitioning at the φ point within the release window.

4. Temperature/pressure inflection: Borehole temperature or pressure sensors should show a detectable inflection point at the φ-predicted minute, marking the transition from accumulation to release.

Section 7
Honest Assessment
Summary: The original 20:1 surface miss was a measurement artifact. When we measured only the surface eruption as the release phase, Old Faithful appeared to violate the φ condition. But geyser physics tells us the eruption process begins far below the surface, minutes before anything is visible.

The underground onset hypothesis predicts the tremor peak timing — 24.9 minutes before surface eruption — that matches Wu et al. 2019 seismic data (~25 min) to within approximately 6 seconds. This reframe does not just rescue Old Faithful; it deepens the framework's conditions by distinguishing between observable surface events and true system-level phase boundaries.

However: this result remains contingent on confirming the tremor peak timing across eruption types (long vs short) with cycle-matched data. The φ prediction of 24.9 min uses the classic Azzalini dataset (mean cycle 74.4 min), while the Wu et al. seismic study may reflect modern cycle lengths (~90–94 min). Full verification requires aligning dataset epochs. The switching rate near-hit (0.631 vs 0.618) now makes additional sense — it may reflect the same φ structure operating in the state-transition dynamics — but remains a single data point of uncertain significance.
