## Step 1: Zenodo (Get Your DOI First)

Do this BEFORE posting anywhere else. Once you have a DOI, every other post can link to it, and it timestamps your work permanently.

1. Go to **zenodo.org** and click "Sign up" — use your GitHub account (it links automatically)
2. Click **"New Upload"** (green button, top right)
3. Upload your **geometry_of_time_preprint.pdf**
4. Fill in the metadata:
   - **Title:** The Geometry of Time — A Heuristic Framework for Non-Equilibrium Phase Ratios
   - **Upload type:** Publication → Preprint
   - **Authors:** La Franchi, Dylan
   - **Description:** Copy the abstract from the paper
   - **Keywords:** golden ratio, phi, relaxation oscillator, non-equilibrium thermodynamics, ARA ratio, phase asymmetry, temporal dynamics
   - **License:** Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 (CC-BY-4.0) — this lets people cite and build on it while crediting you
   - **Related identifiers:** Add your GitHub repo URL as "is supplemented by"
5. Click **"Publish"**
6. You'll get a DOI immediately (format: 10.5281/zenodo.XXXXXXX)
7. **Save this DOI** — you'll add it to everything else

**Time:** ~10 minutes
**Cost:** Free

Before posting anywhere, add a README.md to your repo so people who click through see a clean landing page.


**Time:** ~15 minutes

## Step 3: Substack

1. Go to **substack.com** and create an account (free)
2. Set up a publication — name it something like "Dylan La Franchi" or "Geometry of Time" or whatever feels right
3. Create a new post, paste in the content from **substack_draft.md**
4. Substack uses a rich text editor — the markdown will need light reformatting:
   - Bold text: select and Ctrl+B
   - Headers: use the heading dropdown
   - Links: highlight text, Ctrl+K, paste URL
5. Add your links at the bottom:
   - GitHub repo URL
   - Zenodo DOI link (use the doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.XXXXXXX format)
   - Direct link to the HTML on GitHub
6. **Subtitle suggestion:** "What if every natural cycle follows the same hidden ratio?"
7. Preview it, then **Publish**

**Tips for Substack:**
- Add a header image if you have one (the locomotion arc figure would work well)
- Tag it with relevant topics: science, physics, mathematics, complexity
- Your first post won't have subscribers — that's fine, it's a permanent URL you can share everywhere else

**Time:** ~20 minutes
**Cost:** Free

## Step 4: Reddit

Reddit is where this could get real traction — or get torn apart. Both are good. Target these subreddits in order of priority:

### Tier 1 — Post first (most receptive to independent/exploratory work)

**r/complexity** (~15k members)
- Audience: complexity science enthusiasts and researchers
- Tone: curious, interdisciplinary
- Post as: link post to your Substack or GitHub
- Title suggestion: "I measured the accumulation/release phase ratio across 12 natural systems — they cluster around φ on a 0-to-2 scale (open-source analysis, invitation to falsify)"

**r/NonlinearDynamics** (~5k members)
- Audience: dynamical systems people
- Tone: technical but open
- Post as: link to the HTML framework or Zenodo preprint

### Tier 2 — Post after Tier 1 feedback

**r/physics** (~3M members)
- Audience: broad, includes experts
- Caution: they will be sceptical of golden ratio claims. Lead with methodology and negative controls, not φ
- Title suggestion: "Measuring temporal phase asymmetry across 12 natural systems — a falsifiable classification framework (open-source, preprint)"
- Post on the weekly "What are you working on?" thread first if nervous

**r/biophysics** (~12k members)
- Audience: quantitative biology people
- Angle: lead with the gait results and the Parkinson's finding — that's the strongest empirical piece

**r/datascience** (~2M members)
- Audience: methods-focused
- Angle: lead with the Monte Carlo significance test and the open-source reproducibility angle

### Tier 3 — Broader reach

**r/science** (~32M members)
- Strict rules: requires peer-reviewed sources or preprints with DOI
- Your Zenodo DOI qualifies as a preprint — but read their posting rules carefully
- Flair: "Interdisciplinary" or "Physics"

**r/Biomechanics** (~10k members)
- Angle: the gait locomotion arc specifically

### Reddit posting tips

- **Don't post to all subreddits at once.** Stagger over 1-2 weeks. Cross-posting looks spammy.
- **Be in the comments.** Respond to every critique. Your honesty about limitations is your strongest asset.
- **Never be defensive.** If someone says "this is numerology," respond with "here's the Monte Carlo test, here are the negative controls — happy to discuss where the methodology falls short."
- **Link to the GitHub, not just the paper.** Redditors respect open-source reproducibility more than polished documents.
- **Don't use the words "golden ratio" in the title if posting to physics subs.** Use "phase ratio" or "temporal asymmetry." Let people discover the φ connection in the paper.

**Time:** ~30 minutes per subreddit
**Cost:** Free
