What Is This, in Plain English?

I've been playing with a simple question: when something cycles — a heartbeat, a solar cycle, an ocean current, an earthquake — can a single ratio describe what kind of cycle it is? Not as a metaphor, but as a number you can actually compute and compare across very different systems.

I've been calling this ratio ARA, short for Accumulation / Release Asymmetry. It measures something simple: when a system goes through a cycle (up then down, in then out, build then release), how much longer does it spend building up compared to releasing?

Think of it like this

A wave at the beach rises slowly, then crashes quickly. The time it spends rising vs. crashing is its ARA. A clock pendulum swings left for the same time it swings right — its ARA is roughly 1.0. Your heart spends more time filling than squeezing — that ratio is its ARA too.

The thing I've been chasing is whether this ratio — together with the golden ratio (φ ≈ 1.618), the same number that shows up in spirals and sunflowers — can sort a lot of cycling systems into a small number of categories, and in some cases let you make a useful prediction from very little input. I'm not sure it generalises everywhere it seems to. That's what the testing is for, and I try to share the misses alongside the hits.

When I measure ARA across the systems I've gathered, they tend to sort into three loose groups. I want to be upfront: this is based on the data I've been able to collect, not an exhaustive survey of the universe. But the same three patterns keep showing up in places I didn't expect them to:

Long slow build-up, sudden explosive release. Earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, neural spikes, lightning. Dramatic but unstable.

The middle number — 1.618, called phi (φ) or the golden ratio — isn't picked for prettiness. It's the most "irrational" number in mathematics: the hardest one to approximate with simple fractions. The reason that's interesting for cycles is that a system whose rhythm sits at φ never quite locks into a repeating pattern. It can't deplete its energy in one shared moment. It spreads its energy across every cycle as evenly as possible. That property — established mathematically long before I came along — might be why so many self-sustaining systems seem to settle near it.

How this was worked out

The measurement: ARA is calculated by measuring how long a system spends in its "accumulation" phase (building up) vs. its "release" phase (letting go). For a heartbeat, this is the time the ventricle fills divided by the time it squeezes. For a solar cycle, it's the years spent ramping up to peak activity divided by the years spent declining.

ARA = T_accumulation / T_release

The evidence: Script 40 measured 143 systems across all scales. Biological systems cluster at a mean slope of 1.613 in the Energy-vs-Time power law — just 0.005 away from φ = 1.618. Engineered systems: 1.454. Geophysical: 0.264. Quantum: 0.086. The hierarchy by self-organization level is statistically significant.

Why φ specifically: φ is defined by a simple equation: φ² = φ + 1. Its continued fraction expansion is [1; 1, 1, 1, ...] — all 1s. This makes it the worst approximable irrational number (Hurwitz's theorem). For an oscillator, this means no rational frequency ratio ever matches, so the system never resonates destructively. KAM theory from physics confirms: the φ-torus is the last to break under perturbation. Script 113 verified this — φ-systems retain the highest coherence under noise.

From those four numbers alone — no fitting, no training, no historical data — the formula sketches out a wave. Then, and only then, real data is loaded to compare. This is the strictest test I know how to run on the idea, and I'll be honest later about how often it works and how often it falls flat.

Analogy

Imagine someone hands you one photograph of a river at one moment, tells you the slope of the terrain and the size of the valley, and asks you to sketch the river's likely path for the next 200 miles. You won't get every bend right. The question is whether the overall shape lands close enough to be useful.

I've been thinking of it more as a "what if the shape comes from geometry?" experiment than a finished theory. A working model would say "let me look at the data and find a pattern." This is asking the opposite question: "if a particular geometric structure is real, what should the data look like?" Then I check.

Every oscillating system has a ground cycle — its fundamental rhythm. For a heart, it's the pump beat (~1.6 seconds). For the solar cycle, it's the ~11-year Schwabe cycle. Confirmed (single ENSO dataset)  The "Compass" Approach — Just Predict Direction

I noticed the framework's strongest signal seems to be direction rather than amplitude. So I built a "compass" version that only outputs a sign at each step (up / down) and chains those sign-calls into a wave anchored to the last observation. On NOAA's ENSO data this gives +0.97 correlation and 0.21°C average error at 1-month lead, and +0.93 correlation, 0.45°C error at 3 months. No amplitude prediction needed — the shape of the wave emerges from the chained direction calls. Worth noting: this is one dataset (NINO 3.4 since 1948), and the result needs replication on other oscillating systems before I'd call it general.

How this was worked out

The compass mechanism: at each forecast tick τ:

  1. Compute the framework's amplitude prediction at τ and at τ−1.
  2. Direction = sign of the difference (rising or falling).
  3. Step magnitude = calibrated from training data (mean monthly |Δ|).
  4. Update wave position: wave[τ] = wave[τ−1] + direction × step.
  5. Repeat for each forward tick.

The ensemble compass runs 200 stochastic versions of the compass with sampled direction probabilities and step magnitudes, then takes the ensemble mean. At h=1 this gives the best single forecast we've ever achieved on real ENSO data: corr +0.974, MAE 0.207°C.

Confirmed (cross-domain breakthrough)  Actual-Values Delta-Integration — The Canonical Predictor

The biggest practical advance from this session. The earlier prediction architectures all subtracted the training mean, then summed bandpass cosines back, then added the mean. That averaging across history kept the predictions damped and unable to track local fluctuations. The fix — suggested by Dylan as "we don't work in averages or means, we go for actual values" — reformulates the predictor as a delta integration anchored at the most recent observed value:

v(h) = v_now + Σ_rung amp × (cos(θ + 2π·h/p) − cos(θ))

No training mean. Per-rung amplitude read from the most recent cycle only (peak-to-peak / 2), not averaged over a 50-sample window. Sum (not average) of rung contributions. The cosine-difference form telescopes the per-step delta integration into a closed-form expression — one evaluation per horizon, fast.

Cross-domain results: at h = 1 month on ENSO, MAE drops from 0.63 °C to 0.27 °C (a 57% reduction), correlation +0.65 → +0.93. At h = 1 beat on PhysioNet nsr001 ECG, MAE drops from 117 ms to 19 ms (84% reduction), correlation reaches +0.99. At h = 10 beats the framework beats persistence on this dataset for the first time (R²(persistence) +0.48). Same formula, two systems separated by ~38 orders of φ in time scale.

Released as ara_framework.py — a single Python file, ~250 lines, with the inverse half (extract_topology) and forward half (predict) clearly separated. The forward predictor uses a sigmoid blend between this short-lead form and a structured-wave long-lead form, transitioning at a horizon set by φ±7/4× the system's home period (sign depends on whether the system has a tight matched-rung partner).

How this was worked out

The architectural insight: earlier predictors subtracted training mean, applied averaged amplitudes (std of last 50 bandpass samples) to cosine projections, then re-added the mean. Predictions hugged the mean because the bandpass cosines summed to small values relative to actual signal swings. Anchoring instead at v_now (the actual most-recent value) and integrating deltas keeps the prediction in the data's actual neighbourhood.

Canonical formula (forward half):

extract_topology(data, t): v_now = data[t-1] # actual last value for each pinned rung k: period = φ^k # natural time units bp = causal_bandpass(data[:t], period) amp = (max - min) of last cycle / 2 # not std, not averaged θ = phase from current value + velocity return Topology(v_now, mean_train, home_k, rungs) predict(topo, h, closed): ACT(h) = v_now + Σ amp×(cos(θ+2πh/p) - cos(θ)) OLD(h) = mean + Σ w_k×amp×cos(θ+2πh/p) h_cross = home_period × φ^(±7/4) weight = sigmoid((h_cross - h)/h_cross) return weight×ACT(h) + (1-weight)×OLD(h)

Two regimes, one topology: ACT (delta-integration) wins at short lead because it's anchored at the actual current state. OLD (structured wave from mean) wins at long lead because the cosine term cycles back to v_now whenever h matches a rung period, making ACT lose coherence past the home period.

Crossover empirically falls at φ±7/4× home period: above home for open systems (ECG: ~109 beats vs home 47 beats), below home for closed systems (ENSO: ~8.8 months vs home 47 months). How I'm Trying to Stay Honest

I'm one curious person doing this in spare time. I don't have peer reviewers built in, so I try to keep my own work to the standards I'd want a stranger to hold me to:

Failures stay alongside successes

When a prediction model went wildly unstable (values swinging from −3,000 to +900), I saved it right next to the working version. When a "data leakage" error made results look better than they were, I documented the retraction in the theory document instead of quietly deleting it.

Every claim is labelled by confidence

The longer theory document has three tiers: Section 1 for findings I've checked against real data, Section 2 for patterns that need independent replication, and Section 3 for conceptual ideas that are invitations to think. I try not to blur the boundaries even when something feels exciting.

I rebuild the methodology when I notice it was wrong

For months I used a standard statistical method (leave-one-out cross-validation) that turned out to be the wrong tool for what I was doing. When I caught this, I rebuilt the testing setup from scratch and moved the old scripts to a "superseded" folder. The same thing happened just last week with an acausal filter that was leaking future data. Catching that wiped out a headline result; the corrected number is the one in this document now.

I report numbers as they actually came out

The strictest "four numbers in" version of the formula currently produces correlations between −0.20 and +0.15 against real data. Those are low numbers. I'm reporting them without excuse. The more forgiving framework method does better (+0.76 to +0.86), but it uses training data to learn phase — an easier test. Both numbers, both labels.

What didn't work is documented too

Multiplicative envelopes made the forecast worse. Linear blending of Vehicle + Framework drove the Vehicle's weight to zero. Most events-layer basis functions overfit and didn't generalise. Amplitude reach caps at about 36% of the real range using the framework's own constants. I keep these around as guardrails so I don't repeat them.

I'm not a physicist, a cardiologist, a climate scientist, or a cosmologist. I live with ME/CFS and have a lot of thinking time. The strongest version of this work would be when people in the relevant fields look at it and either improve it, knock pieces of it down, or show me what I'm missing. Why I Keep Coming Back to This

Most readers won't run the scripts or check the math. So here's what keeps me at it, in plain language:

Things cycle. Hearts, breath, energy through a day, seasons, the way conversations build and release tension. The question I started with was whether the same simple ratio could describe a lot of these very different cycles. So far the data I've gathered keeps suggesting yes — in a soft, statistical way, not a perfect-law way.

Balance might be measurable. If the cluster around φ in biological data is real, then how far a system has drifted from that ratio could be a useful number. I'm not yet confident enough to call it a diagnostic, but I think it's worth checking.

Things are connected in ways I can put numbers on. Not in a mystical sense — in the dull arithmetic sense that systems at different scales tend to share structural features I can measure. That's modest as a claim, but it's the kind of modest claim I can defend.

The bigger question I'm circling, and that I'd love smarter people to take seriously, is whether there's a single geometric principle underlying many natural cycles. I don't know. The evidence I've gathered so far is suggestive in some places and inconclusive in others. I've tried to be transparent about which is which.

Where Things Stand Right Now

Updated May 2026 — canonical predictor consolidated, 3/4 ceiling falsification test passed, conceptual extensions opened

Earlier in May it looked like a stack of framework refinements was reaching 89% direction and +0.75 correlation at 2-year ENSO lead. When I went back and validated more carefully, with a strict-causal filter instead of an acausal FFT bandpass, most of that lift turned out to be feature leakage. The filter at time t was using data from after t. I caught it myself, retracted those numbers, and rebuilt from the strict-causal protocol. That set the rules for everything since.

The biggest single advance during the rebuild was reformulating the predictor in actual values rather than deviations-from-mean. Anchoring at the most recent observed value and integrating deltas forward dropped 1-month ENSO MAE from 0.63 °C to 0.27 °C and 1-beat ECG MAE from 117 ms to 19 ms. Same formula on both. The predictor is released as the single Python file ara_framework.py, with the inverse half (extracting topology coordinates) and forward half (predicting from those coordinates) cleanly separated.

The findings that survived the strict-causal rebuild and matter most:

  • The canonical predictor. One formula, two regimes (short-lead delta-integration, long-lead structured-wave), blended by a sigmoid at φ±7/4× home period. Cross-domain confirmed on ENSO + ECG. Sign of the 7/4 exponent appears to be set by whether the system has a tight matched-rung partner.
  • Vertical-ARA across 4 mammals. Mouse, rabbit, dog and human all share the same local cycle shape after rescaling time by their own heart period. All six pairwise correlations within ±2 rungs of peak land between +0.89 and +1.00 (mean +0.955).
  • Walker Circulation is fractal across rungs. Dynamic per-rung partner discovery shows SOI mirrors NINO anti-phase from φ5 to φ11 with |corr| ≥ 0.85 throughout. The matched-rung pair sits at the partner's dominant rung, not necessarily at the target's home rung.
  • Closed-system coupling differs from incidental coupling. SOI as a matched-rung partner at φ8 fixed the mid-horizon ENSO dip; the same SOI treated as an incidental feeder did nothing.
  • Framework constants port across domains. The lag-h corrector with γ ≈ 1/φ lifts MAE 37% on ECG at 1 minute and 17% on ENSO at 24 months. Same constant, same sign rule, two unrelated systems.
  • Mid-horizon dips are signatures of intruder waves. The framework's prediction breakdown at ~600 beats on ECG (8 of 11 subjects) coincides with mid-frequency autonomic transitions. Not a bug — the architecture being faithful to a wave from another system passing through.
